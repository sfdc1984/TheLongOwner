Hidden Honda Decisions

The Alert That Only Shows Up When I'm Actually Distracted

A small traffic-jam feature, and why it only ever seems to matter in one specific situation.

Observation

I noticed the alert had a pattern.

Sitting in bumper-to-bumper city traffic, glancing at my phone or checking a mirror for a moment too long, I'd occasionally get a prompt telling me the vehicle ahead had moved. What struck me wasn't the alert itself — it was that it never once appeared on the open highway, and it never appeared when I was already alert and rolling forward with traffic.

Engineering Explained

What is "Vehicle Ahead Has Moved"?

This is a low-speed traffic-assist alert. It watches the gap to the car in front, and if that gap opens up and you don't respond within a short window, it prompts you. It's designed specifically for stop-and-go crawl, not for cruising — which is exactly why it only ever seems to fire in the situation where a driver's attention is most likely to have drifted.

Based on my own observation, it appears calibrated for the moment your mind wanders, not the moment you're already engaged — though I haven't cross-checked the exact trigger logic against Honda's own documentation, so this remains an informed hypothesis rather than a confirmed spec.

DriveLab Insight
This isn't a feature for the highway. It's a feature for the ten seconds your mind wanders in a traffic jam.
Try It Yourself

In your next stretch of slow city traffic, notice whether the alert only ever appears when you were genuinely distracted — phone, mirror, conversation — versus when you were already watching the road. That pattern is the clearest evidence of how it's actually calibrated.
